## Overview

Presented at the **DOE Community College Internship (CCI) Program**, supported by the U.S. Department of Energy, Office of Science.

Co-authored with Stijn Wielandt, Ivo Markovic, and Robin Thibaut at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory.

As 5G technology advances, its benefits remain largely confined to urban areas. Remote and challenging terrain still lacks the infrastructure needed for real-time data transmission. This project addresses that gap by designing a rapidly deployable outdoor data collection system that pairs a **Raspberry Pi** with a **Quectel RM502Q-AE 5G cellular module**, enabling low-cost, real-time geospatial data transmission from areas with limited or no connectivity.

![Presenting the project poster at the DOE Community College Internship Program symposium](/works/kriging/poster-presentation.jpg)

## Hardware System

The sensor unit at the heart of the system is a Raspberry Pi paired with the RM502Q-AE 5G cellular module. Fitted with external antennas for maximum signal reception, the unit collects RSSI values and GPS coordinates — including altitude — and transmits them in real time to a central server over the 5G link. Its compact form factor makes it easy to carry into the field and position at any measurement point.

![Raspberry Pi with RM502Q-AE 5G module deployed at the field site](/works/kriging/raspi-field.jpg)

The full signal tower scales this capability up for stationary, long-range deployments. A lightweight foldable tripod base supports a mast carrying a dish antenna, directional antenna, and Starlink terminal. All structural components use PVC pipe fittings, which are cheap, easy to cut, weatherproof, and RF-transparent — an intentional material choice to avoid interfering with the antennas.

![PVC cross-junction used for mounting and routing antenna cables through the tower mast](/works/kriging/antenna-mount.jpg)

Power distribution and electronics are housed in a ruggedized waterproof case at the base of the tower. Inside, terminal blocks, a relay, and a custom PCB manage power routing from the battery to the Raspberry Pi, antennas, and 5G module. The enclosure keeps all electronics protected during multi-day outdoor deployments.

![Interior of the power and electronics control box, showing terminal blocks, relay, and custom PCB](/works/kriging/electronics-box.jpg)

## Kriging Algorithm

The analytical pipeline takes raw CSV measurements as input and produces a continuous signal strength map as a GeoTIFF. The flowchart below illustrates the full processing sequence: GPS coordinates are first converted to UTM13N, a prediction grid is generated over the survey area, an experimental variogram is computed and fitted to a parametric model, and Ordinary Kriging is performed across the grid. If the variogram fit is poor, the model is swapped and the process repeats before proceeding to kriging.

![Kriging algorithm logic map — from CSV input through variogram fitting to GeoTIFF output](/works/kriging/flowchart.png)

Variogram fitting uses **PyTorch neural networks** with bounded parameter optimization, evaluating spherical, exponential, and Gaussian models and selecting the best fit by minimizing weighted mean absolute error across lag distances. A convex hull mask limits estimation to regions covered by actual observations, reducing extrapolation error. Elevation is sampled from a DEM file via `rasterio` to account for 3D terrain effects in the kriging system.

## Results

The final output is a georeferenced RSSI coverage map overlaid on satellite imagery in QGIS, with topographic contours showing how terrain elevation correlates with signal strength. Warm colors (red/orange) indicate strong signal near the tower; cool colors (blue/purple) show attenuation at range. The colored dots are the raw measurement points collected during the field survey.
