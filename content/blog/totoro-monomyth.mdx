*If one day, there is no one left in this world to care for you. When you are sad, remember there are still clouds in the sky shedding tears for you; when you are discouraged, there are still flowers by the roadside cheering you on.*

The seemingly contrasting terms of fantasy and reality are vividly portrayed as one in Hayao Miyazaki's film *My Neighbor Totoro*. In this seemingly ordinary and uneventful daily life, the movie unfolds a grand and sweeping Joseph Campbell-esque hero's journey. *My Neighbor Totoro* uses the monomyth to teach us to possess eyes that discover curiosity, legs that can tread through hardships, hands that can nurture innocence, a chest that can melt fear, and a heart that can embrace growth.

## I. Call to Adventure — Eyes That Discover Magic

*My Neighbor Totoro* uses the Call to Adventure stage to teach us to always possess a pair of eyes that allow us to discover the magic around us.

When Mei and Satsuki moved to the countryside due to their mother's illness and settled into a broken "haunted house," they did not feel down by the sudden change. Instead, along with their eyes fueled by curiosity, the scattered acorns on the ground became evidence of squirrels and Totoro's presence. The dusty loft served as the habitat for dust elves. Facing a storm, they laughed in the face of fear, driving away the temporary guests of Yokais. What led them into their wonderland was not a destiny suddenly dropped from the sky or a task passed upon them by blood, but the anticipation in their eyes for magic — transforming the simple into their realm, planting wonders into the frame of everyday life.

In *This is Water*, David Foster Wallace says, "It is about simple awareness — awareness of what is so real and essential, so hidden in plain sight all around us, that we have to keep reminding ourselves, over and over: 'This is water, this is water'" (8). Water surrounds the fish, every moment, simple and natural, so much so that we never notice its presence. In our childhood, we too would play like Mei and Satsuki, get dirty, laugh off fears. Each person's childhood is their fairy tale — not made by the experiences of childhood, but because our eyes enable us to see a world of wonders.

As one grows, people sacrifice what they once thought unimportant for pursuits they consider more valuable. The experiences of Mei and Satsuki highlight how steep this cost is, and how important it is to be mindful of oneself at every moment — cherishing every second in one's surroundings that make us see the different side of the world with enjoyment and passion.

## II. Meeting with the Goddess — Hands That Nurture Innocence

*My Neighbor Totoro* uses the Meeting with the Goddess stage to teach us to possess hands filled with optimism, embracing and nurturing fantasies.

When Mei and Satsuki received seeds from Totoro, they joyfully planted them and prayed for them to sprout. One night, the Totoro family arrived, and through Totoro's sweat and determined expression, the seeds grew into towering trees. With the sisters aboard, they soared in a graceful arc towards the treetops, gazing into the future. The next day, the seeds had sprouted — and a drawing of the sapling on the father's work documents conveyed the identity of the Totoro who had performed the magic at night. Just as the father cycled with the sisters drawing arcs as they arrived in the countryside, he continued to draw arcs, carrying the aspirations of the family, raising the seeds, and soaring towards the future at the top of the tree.

In *Catching the Big Fish*, David Lynch writes, "But if you can expand that consciousness, make it grow, then when you read that book, you'll have more understanding; when you look out, more awareness; when you wake up, more wakefulness; and as you go about your day, more inner happiness" (1). Understanding the deep does not erase the significance of the surface — interpreting the shallow consciousness with the deeper is the most crucial part.

As one begins to grow, it means breaking through reality to accept the responsibilities of growth, and the eyes of imagination are lost, as well as the trace of Totoro. When the father learns that his daughters long for fantasies, he becomes the one who constructs them, nurturing innocence. Because only kids can see Totoro — and only adults can become Totoro.

## III. Road of Trials — A Chest That Melts Fear

*My Neighbor Totoro* uses the Road of Trials stage to lead us to always possess a chest to burn the dread in our life as the inevitables come.

On the first day the family moves into their new house, a storm arrives, causing Mei and Satsuki to be very frightened. However, the father laughs heartily, saying that as long as you laugh, scary things will run away — so the family starts laughing together in the face of the storm as the dust elves hiding on the roof quietly slip away. Dust elves only appear in houses that are uninhabited. But a more accurate description is that they emerge when there is no passionate soul residing in the house.

In *The Power of Myth*, Joseph Campbell says, "It erases anxieties, it puts them in accord with the inevitables of their life, and they can see the positive values of what are the negative aspects of what is positive" (1). A braver person is not someone who never feels fear, but rather someone who dares to confront fears and overcome them.

Encouraged by their father, Mei and Satsuki — despite losing their mother's presence at such a young age — continue to view the world with innocence, passion, and positivity. As the storm comes again, they are no longer afraid. The shy boy Kanta blushingly lends his umbrella to the sisters; the Catbus allays their fears at the bus station. The Totoros give courage to those with the will to stand through difficulty, and as the sisters hand the umbrella to their father, they give him that same courage. This time, Satsuki and Mei have become the little Totoros for their father.

## IV. The Ultimate Boon — Legs That Tread Through Hardship

*My Neighbor Totoro* uses the Ultimate Boon stage to guide us to always possess a pair of feet that can face and tread through challenges.

When the sisters learned that their mother was in a precarious situation, Satsuki insisted on staying put to avoid causing more trouble, while Mei desperately wanted to go to the hospital to see her mother. Satsuki harshly rebuked Mei, and the strained relationship between the sisters led Mei to embark on the journey to the hospital alone. Yet Satsuki was not without worry — she cried and confided in the neighbor's grandmother, expressing her fears about what would happen if their mother didn't make it. The sisters shared the same concerns, but their sense of responsibility led them down different paths.

In *This is Water*, David Foster Wallace writes, "The really important kind of freedom involves attention, and awareness, and discipline, and effort, and being able truly to care about other people and to sacrifice for them, over and over, in myriad petty little unsexy ways, every day — that is real freedom" (8).

Mei and Satsuki represent different stages of the same person's growth. When Mei's childlike innocence prompts her to pursue her desires, the mature Satsuki steps forward to hide her individual desires for the greater good. However, the purpose of growth is neither blindly following one's heart nor completely submerging oneself in the whirlpool of responsibilities — but finding harmony between the two. As Satsuki searches for Mei, she borrows the strength from innocence, from Totoro. And upon hearing her sister's voice, the lost Mei begins to accept her own responsibilities. At the end, the sisters collaborate wholeheartedly to reach the place they yearn for. Upon meeting their longed-for mother and cheerful father, they decide not to enter — leaving only the corn behind as they depart.

True freedom is never petty or unsexy, nor is it only achieved by sacrificing oneself for others. Life's hardships may impede you, but there is no need to abandon your true self. Acting true to oneself, without betraying one's own values for the sake of others, is the essence of real freedom.

## V. Crossing the Return Threshold — A Heart That Embraces Growth

*My Neighbor Totoro* uses the Crossing the Return Threshold stage to guide us to always possess a heart that never fears the future and faces growth.

Upon seeing their mother, the sisters rode the Catbus back home. What truly marked their return was when they bid farewell to the Catbus. Instead of walking away, the Catbus vanished — signifying that Mei and Satsuki are also on the brink of growing up. They might never see Totoro again, because they are about to embark on a journey towards the real Totoros in their lives: father, the neighbor's grandmother, Kanta, and the villagers who helped them — these are the Yokais of their future.

In *The Hero's Journey*, Lynne Milum writes, "The narrative now brings the hero full cycle — it is his destiny to depart from the mystical world he has discovered, and return to the banalities of life with his bounty" (4).

The story of Totoro is a gentle legacy. Those who have encountered Totoro as children will understand how to become Totoro themselves. Because they know that even if fairy tales are not real, the yearning they once had is true, the love in their hearts is real, the colorful childhood is undoubtable, and the joyful memories will be immortal. Mei and Satsuki may one day grow up, get married, raise children, and eventually age without seeing Totoro ever again. However, the unchanged essence of their childhood — having once encountered Totoro — will preserve a pure and childlike heart that is indestructible no matter what comes ahead. One day, when their own children tell them about meeting Totoro, Mei and Satsuki will smile and say: *"Really? I saw Totoro when I was a child too!"*

---

*My Neighbor Totoro* is a work that beautifully captures the essence of imagination. You won't know who Totoro is, or where it comes from, but that's not important. What matters is not whether Totoro truly exists, but whether you are able to see Totoro.

After watching the movie, you'll realize that Totoro does indeed exist. Those who help us in life — they are just like the adorable Totoros in the forest. And after being saved, we too can become someone else's Totoro. The world has always been a place filled with fairy tales, and the so-called Threshold is merely whether you are willing to discover it or not.

If I could meet my 6-year-old self, I would tell him not to rush to grow up and to enjoy the present. If I could meet my 14-year-old self, I would tell him not to fear growing up and to face it with a childlike heart. And now, I would tell my present self on behalf of my future self: continue walking both as a child and as an adult.

When you face difficulties one day, believe that the rain is the clouds shedding tears for you, the sun is the flowers cheering you on, the bus is a cat with eight legs, and your neighbor will forever be the Totoro smiling at you. When you reach the halfway point in life, coming back will still find you as a youth.
